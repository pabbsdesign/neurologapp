# Methodology

## 1. Attribution Model

The three-condition attribution model assigns a weighted score to each logged symptom based on its known clinical association with idiopathic intracranial hypertension (IIH), Chiari malformation type 1, and chronic migraine respectively. When a symptom is logged, the model distributes a score across all three conditions in proportion to these weights, producing a running estimate of which condition is most likely contributing to the current symptom burden. The weights were derived from the lived diagnostic experience of the developer combined with published symptom profiles for each condition. They represent a hypothesis to be validated rather than a clinically verified scoring system, and they should be read accordingly. The weights are configurable in the `SYMPTOMS` constant and can be adjusted to reflect alternative clinical interpretations or to adapt the model to a different condition profile.

## 2. Resilience Gap

The Resilience Gap is a metric that compares objective functional impact scores against psychological response scores. Functional impact is measured using the HIT-6 (Headache Impact Test) and the BPI-SF (Brief Pain Inventory — Short Form), both of which quantify how pain and headache impair daily functioning. Psychological response is measured using the PASS-20 (Pain Anxiety Symptoms Scale) and the PCS-12 (Pain Catastrophising Scale), which capture the cognitive and emotional reactions to pain. The gap is calculated as the numerical difference between these two dimensions. It is intended to identify situations where perceived distress appears disproportionate to, or consistent with, the measurable physical impact of symptoms — and to do so in a way that makes that comparison explicit and auditable rather than implied. This is a novel metric not derived from any existing validated framework and is presented as a hypothesis for clinical evaluation rather than an established analytical tool.

## 3. Origin and Development Process

The selection and combination of clinical instruments used in this tool was developed through iterative conversations with an AI assistant (Claude, Anthropic). In those conversations the developer reasoned through their chronic pain symptom profile, explored which validated instruments might meaningfully track symptoms across three overlapping conditions, and worked through the question of how to distinguish condition-specific pain from generalised distress. The instrument set was not assembled from a pre-existing protocol but emerged from a process of structured reasoning about what the data would need to capture and why.

A specific motivating factor in the development of the Resilience Gap metric was the developer's experience of receiving a somatic symptom disorder label in a clinical setting where physically observed symptoms were present. The metric was designed in part to create a structured, quantitative way of examining the relationship between physical and psychological dimensions rather than assuming psychological primacy. The intent is not to contest the clinical utility of psychological assessment in chronic pain but to provide a framework in which that relationship can be examined from the data rather than presupposed.

The process described above — a patient using conversational AI to develop clinical reasoning they could not readily access through formal channels — is itself documented here as a potentially novel development methodology. The use of AI-assisted reasoning to bridge the gap between patient experience and clinical instrument selection, in the context of a contested or uncertain diagnosis, may be worth research attention in its own right, independent of the specific tool that resulted from it.

## 4. Validation Status

No clinical validation has been performed on either the attribution model or the Resilience Gap metric. Both are patient-derived methodologies based on a single condition profile — IIH, probable Chiari malformation type 1, and chronic migraine — developed by one person about their own symptom presentation. The attribution weights have not been tested against independent clinical datasets, and the Resilience Gap formula has not been evaluated against established psychological or functional outcome measures in a research setting. This tool is shared as a reference implementation and a starting point for research interest, not as a validated clinical instrument. It should not be used as the basis for clinical decision-making without independent validation.

## 5. Limitations

The tool is currently hardcoded for one specific condition profile and one specific medication regimen, which limits its direct applicability to other patients without modification. The attribution weights have not been tested across multiple patients with the same or similar diagnoses and may not generalise beyond the individual case from which they were derived. The Resilience Gap comparison assumes that the selected functional impact scales and the selected psychological response scales are sufficiently independent to produce a meaningful differential; this assumption has not been formally tested, and there may be measurement overlap between the instruments that would confound the gap calculation. The AI-assisted reasoning process that informed instrument selection has not been independently reviewed for clinical accuracy, and there is no guarantee that the instrument combinations chosen represent best practice from a psychometric or clinical measurement standpoint.

## 6. Intended Research Use

The methodology described in this document is offered for validation, for stress-testing of the weighting model against broader patient datasets, and for exploration of whether the attribution framework could be reconfigured for other neurological condition clusters where overlapping and difficult-to-disaggregate symptom presentations are a known clinical challenge. The Resilience Gap in particular is offered as a candidate metric for evaluation — researchers with access to appropriate datasets may be in a position to assess whether the functional-psychological differential it captures correlates with clinically meaningful outcomes.

The somatisation context in which this tool was developed is offered as an additional dimension of research interest. The broader question of how patients with physically observed symptoms navigate diagnostic environments where psychological attribution is applied, and what tools might support a more structured examination of that relationship, sits at the intersection of clinical measurement, patient advocacy, and diagnostic practice. The AI-assisted development process through which this tool was created — a patient constructing clinical reasoning iteratively with a language model — is offered as a further dimension worth examining, both for what it suggests about the accessibility of clinical knowledge and for what it reveals about the gaps that process is filling.
